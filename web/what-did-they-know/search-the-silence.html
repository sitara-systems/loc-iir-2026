Climate change didn't appear in 1988. It didn't appear in 1965. The American archive has held the evidence for nearly 150 years. Search it yourself — and watch the moment the record goes quiet.

1856 – 1913

In 1856, an American woman named Eunice Newton Foote demonstrated that carbon dioxide traps heat. In 1896, Svante Arrhenius calculated that doubling atmospheric CO₂ would warm the Earth by roughly 5°C.

These were not secrets. Newspapers covered them.

Chronicling America · 1856–1913

Small-town papers. Regional dailies. The science of atmospheric warming appeared in newspapers from Iowa to Alaska — decades before any government agency took notice.

By the 1930s, the Dust Bowl was reshaping the continent. Guy Callendar proved in 1938 that CO₂ was already rising and temperatures were climbing. Were Americans being told?

Chronicling America · 1920–1940
1955 – 1965

Now the critical decade. Roger Revelle calls it a "large-scale geophysical experiment." Keeling begins measuring CO₂ at Mauna Loa. The science is hardening into consensus.

And look what the archive holds: an Evening Star article from 1958 headlined "Mystery of the Warming World" — an explicit explanation of global warming, seven years before the President was told.

Chronicling America · 1955–1965
For 109 years — from Eunice Foote's experiment in 1856 to the Johnson administration in 1965 — the archive held the evidence freely. Newspapers printed it. The public could find it.

Then the government catches up. In 1965, the President's Science Advisory Committee delivers a 317-page report warning Lyndon Johnson about CO₂. He transmits it to Congress.

You would expect coverage to increase. Watch what happens instead.

Before 1965, Americans could find climate science in their local paper. After 1965 — after the government officially acknowledged the danger — the coverage nearly vanished.

Social scientists call this pluralistic ignorance — when everyone in a chain assumes someone else is communicating the danger. Scientists assume the press will cover it. The press assumes the government will act on it. The government assumes the public already knows. No one speaks. The record holds the proof.

The archive held the knowledge for 132 years. It also held the silence. Both are in the same building.
1988

On June 23, 1988, James Hansen tells Congress that global warming has already begun. He is 99% certain. The press is in the room. The front pages carry it.

Hansen didn't discover climate change. He said out loud what the record had held for 132 years.

What Did They Know? is an archival investigation that reads the American record against itself — the newspapers that covered it freely, the government reports that finally confirmed it, and the silence that fell in between.
