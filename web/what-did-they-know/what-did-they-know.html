What Did We Know?

The Library of Congress holds the answer.

Climate change was named, measured, and published in American newspapers and scientific journals starting in the 1850s. The Library of Congress holds over 150 years of evidence that we knew.

Chapter I

The Science Emerges

For over a century, the evidence assembled itself — in laboratories, in weather stations, in newspaper columns, and in photographs of a landscape coming apart. All of it eventually filed in the Library of Congress.

1856

An American scientist fills glass cylinders with different gases, places them in sunlight, and measures their temperatures.

The cylinder filled with carbon dioxide heats faster and stays hot longer than any of the others.

Eunice Newton Foote, 1856. The first demonstration that CO2 traps heat.
American Journal of Science, 1856
1856
Eunice Newton Foote

Her name was Eunice Newton Foote. She was a physicist, an inventor, and a signatory of the Seneca Falls Declaration on women's rights. Her paper — "Circumstances Affecting the Heat of the Sun's Rays" — was read aloud at the American Association for the Advancement of Science by a male colleague, because she was not permitted to present.

The paper described the first experimental evidence that carbon dioxide traps heat. It was published in the American Journal of Science and Arts. That was 1856. Abraham Lincoln had not yet been nominated for president.

The first person to demonstrate the greenhouse effect was an American woman who was not allowed to present her own findings.
1856 → 1896
Forty years pass.

Foote's experiment is largely forgotten. In 1861, the Irish physicist John Tyndall publishes more detailed measurements of how gases absorb heat — he is usually credited as the discoverer. Foote's priority is not widely recognized until the 2010s.

Then, in 1896, a Swedish chemist picks up where she left off.

1896

Svante Arrhenius publishes the first mathematical prediction of what fossil fuel combustion will do to the planet.

His estimate — roughly 5°C of warming if atmospheric CO2 doubles — is within the range of modern climate models, 130 years later.

Placeholder
The Leon Reporter, Leon, Iowa, March 24, 1910.
Chronicling America
March 24, 1910
Leon, Iowa. Population: 1,891.

A weekly newspaper in a farming town in southern Iowa publishes a short item about carbon dioxide and the atmosphere. The theory that burning coal could change the Earth's temperature has made it from a Swedish chemistry journal to page 3 of the Leon Reporter.

It's 1910. The Wright brothers flew seven years ago. There are two million cars in the United States. And a weekly in Iowa is printing about CO2.

July 27, 1913
San Francisco, California.

The San Francisco Call runs a science column on page 7 covering the link between coal combustion, carbon dioxide, and temperature. One year earlier, a New Zealand newspaper had published a now-famous article on the same subject — sometimes cited as "the 1912 climate article."

The knowledge is circulating. Unevenly, inconsistently, but circulating — in newspapers that most Americans will never read, on pages they'll turn past on the way to the sports scores.

June 6, 1931
Seward, Alaska.

The Seward Daily Gateway — a newspaper in a town of 800 people on the coast of Alaska — publishes a dispatch about carbon dioxide and atmospheric warming.

Alaska. 1931. A territory, not yet a state. And someone is reading about what CO2 does to the atmosphere.

Dozens of newspaper pages between 1896 and 1940 mention carbon dioxide and warming. Scattered across small-town papers from Iowa to Alaska. The knowledge was in the public record.
1936

While federal photographers are documenting the Dust Bowl, a British engineer is using American weather data to prove the warming is already happening.

The photographs and the proof are being produced at the same time.

Placeholder
Dust storm, Cimarron County, Oklahoma, 1936. Arthur Rothstein / FSA.
FSA Photograph
April 1936
A farmer walks into a dust storm with his two sons.

Arthur Rothstein took this photograph in Cimarron County, Oklahoma. The soil had been stripped by a decade of industrial farming and drought. It was blowing away. The image became one of the defining photographs of the twentieth century.

The Dust Bowl displaced 2.5 million people. It was a climate disaster — drought, heat, soil collapse — before anyone used that language.

1936
They were climate refugees before the term existed.

Dorothea Lange photographed Oklahoma families arriving in Blythe, California. Walker Evans documented eroded farmland in Alabama. Together with Rothstein and others, they produced 175,000 photographs — the most comprehensive visual record of environmental devastation in American history.

Decades later, climate scientists would point to the Dust Bowl as a preview of what warming would bring: more drought, more displacement, more soil turning to dust.

1938
Guy Callendar proves it.

While the Dust Bowl is still burying farms, a British steam engineer named Guy Stewart Callendar compiles temperature records from 147 weather stations around the world — many of them operated by the U.S. Weather Bureau.

His conclusion: the planet had warmed by 0.3°C since the 1880s, and the cause was carbon dioxide from burning coal and oil. He publishes in 1938. The paper is received politely and mostly ignored.

The photographer and the engineer never met. But the federal weather stations that tracked the Dust Bowl storms also supplied the data that proved the warming was real.

The photographs. The weather data. The mathematical proof. All produced in the same decade, all eventually filed in the same archive. And then — eighty years of separation. The photos went to the picture files. The weather data went to the agencies. The proof went to the journals. Nobody read them together.

1957

Roger Revelle declares that humanity is running a "large scale geophysical experiment" on the atmosphere. Charles Keeling begins measuring CO2 at Mauna Loa.

And in Washington, a newspaper is paying attention.

Placeholder
Evening Star, Washington DC, January 26, 1958, page 23.
Chronicling America
January 26, 1958
"Mystery of the Warming World"

Page 23 of the Evening Star — Washington DC's daily newspaper, the paper read by members of Congress, by agency officials, by the staffers who would draft the PSAC report seven years later.

A feature by John Stark. The headline: "Mystery of the Warming World." An article explaining, clearly and explicitly, that carbon dioxide from fossil fuels was heating the planet.

Seven years before the warning reached the White House, it was on page 23 of the Washington paper — between the department store ads and the TV listings.
1955–1965
The coverage begins to thin.

In the decade before the government was formally warned, newspaper coverage of climate science in the digitized record narrows considerably. Among the papers still covering it: the Atlanta Daily World in 1956 and the Jackson Advocate in Mississippi in 1957 — Black newspapers covering climate science during Jim Crow.

Why did coverage thin? Was it editorial priorities, the limits of what Chronicling America has digitized from this period, or something else? That question is one of the things this project is designed to explore.

Chapter II

The Government Responds

In 1965, what the newspapers had been covering for a century reaches the President's desk.

Placeholder
PSAC Report, November 1965. 317 pages. Delivered to President Johnson.
Congressional Record
November 1965
317 pages.

Restoring the Quality of Our Environment. The President's Science Advisory Committee delivers its report. A 22-page appendix on atmospheric CO2 — chaired by Roger Revelle, the same scientist who had raised the alarm a decade earlier — predicts a 25% increase in atmospheric CO2 by the year 2000.

The prediction is almost exactly right.

The report is transmitted to Congress. Johnson sends an accompanying special message on conservation. Everything Foote demonstrated in 1856, everything Arrhenius calculated in 1896, everything Callendar proved in 1938 — it is now an official warning on the desk of the President of the United States.

109 years separate Eunice Foote's experiment from the presidential warning. The Library's collections hold evidence from every decade in between.
February 8, 1965
And then what?

The President sends a special message to Congress. His own scientists have warned him. He transmits their findings to the legislative branch.

In the surviving digitized newspaper record, coverage of this moment is sparse. A century of evidence reaches the White House — and the trail in Chronicling America goes quiet.

What happened to the public conversation after the science entered the congressional record? That's the research question at the heart of this project.
19661987

What happened next?

The government kept studying the problem. The Charney Report in 1979. The EPA warning in 1983. Congressional hearings. Agency reports. The institutional record grew.

But in Chronicling America, newspaper coverage of climate science drops sharply after the mid-1960s and does not return until 1988.

What accounts for this gap? Chronicling America's digitization thins after approximately 1963, which means part of the pattern could reflect what has been digitized rather than what was published. Distinguishing between these is one of the core research questions of this project — and one the Library of Congress is uniquely positioned to help answer, because the physical newspapers and microfilm are in the building.

How did the public encounter — or fail to encounter — what its own institutions already knew?

Chapter III

The Public Record Returns

June 23, 1988. The hottest day of the year.

Placeholder
Senate hearing room, June 23, 1988.
Congressional Record
June 23, 1988
James Hansen says it out loud.

Senator Tim Wirth schedules the hearing for what he expects to be the hottest day of the year. The night before, he has the windows opened so the air conditioning can't keep up.

James Hansen, a NASA climatologist, tells the Senate Committee on Energy and Natural Resources that global warming has already begun. He is 99% certain.

The science he presents is not new. It is what Foote demonstrated in 1856, what Arrhenius calculated in 1896, what Callendar proved in 1938, what the PSAC told Johnson in 1965 — updated with 23 more years of data confirming the prediction. But this time the press is in the room. This time, the front pages carry it.

Hansen didn't discover climate change. He said out loud what the record had held for 132 years.
1856 → 1988
132 years.

From Eunice Foote's glass cylinders to James Hansen's Senate testimony. From a woman who wasn't allowed to present her own paper to a man on the front page of the New York Times.

Between them: a century and a third of evidence. Weather records, newspaper columns, photographs, journal articles, government reports. All of it filed, cataloged, preserved — and now, for the first time, read together.

The Library of Congress holds this entire record. The question this project explores is not whether the science was known — the archive proves it was. The question is what the record can tell us about how that knowledge moved through American public life.

Over 150 years of evidence. All in the same building. Read together for the first time.
2026

What Did We Know? is an archival investigation told through the documents themselves — an 1856 experiment, small-town newspapers, Dust Bowl photographs, a presidential report, and the question of what happened to public awareness in between.

The story is about what was always there in the Library's collections, waiting to be read together for the first time.

A Proposal for the Library of Congress
Innovator in Residence Program

What Did We Know? proposes a scrollytelling experience built from congressional records, digitized newspapers, and federal photographs — reading the Library's collections together to reveal over 150 years of climate evidence and explore how that knowledge moved through American public life.
