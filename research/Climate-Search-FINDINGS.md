# LC Climate Search — Findings
*Generated from 45 keyword searches · loc.gov API + Chronicling America · 2026-03-27*

---

## Summary

**44/45 searches returned results · 1 timed out (E1-06)**

The LC's digital holdings are deep on the congressional/institutional side and almost completely silent on the newspaper/public awareness side. This asymmetry is the analytical finding: the federal government generated and received detailed climate warnings across decades, while public awareness — as measured by newspaper coverage — was effectively zero until 1988.

## Key Findings by Era

### ERA 1: Early Science (1856–1938) — 6/7 returned results

**Eunice Foote (E1-01, E1-02):** 1,183 and 2,057 results respectively, but the top hits are noise — genealogy records, phone directories, general women's biography volumes. Foote's actual 1856 paper does not surface as a discrete LC item. This is significant: the first person to demonstrate CO2 traps heat is not keyword-discoverable at LC. Her paper was published in *American Journal of Science and Arts* (E1-03 confirms LC holds this journal — 17,856 results for the title), but the specific volume containing her work doesn't surface. This is a Eunice Foote–shaped hole in the catalog.

**Arrhenius (E1-04):** 60 results. Top hits are chemistry textbooks and encyclopedia entries, not the 1896 paper itself. LC holds the ecosystem of materials around Arrhenius's work but the paper is not individually cataloged as a discrete item.

**Callendar (E1-05):** 49 results. Same pattern — fuel/thermodynamics textbooks, not the 1938 paper. The primary archive is in the UK.

**Chronicling America 1896–1940 (E1-07):** 62 results. This is promising — **the OCR snippets from the Evening Star (DC) and San Francisco Call suggest there *was* some early press awareness of atmospheric CO2 science, scattered across local papers.** These need manual inspection to confirm they're genuine climate-related articles and not false keyword hits.

**Coal/climate 1890–1915 (E1-06):** Timed out. Re-run with simplified query.

### ERA 2: Mid-Century Knowledge (1950s–1964) — 6/6 returned results

**Roger Revelle (E2-01):** Only 16 results, but the Evening Star (Washington DC) hits from 1955, 1958 are potentially significant — DC's major newspaper covering a local scientist's work on CO2/oceans during the IGY period. Need manual inspection.

**International Geophysical Year (E2-02):** 405 results. Top hits are **congress.gov bills** — congressional resolutions honoring the IGY's anniversary. This confirms IGY is well-documented in the congressional record. The original 1957 appropriations debates should be deeper in these results.

**Keeling (E2-03):** 49 results. Top hits are modern NASA lectures and educational materials, not the original 1958 measurements. The Keeling Curve data itself is at Scripps/NOAA, not LC. But LC holds lectures *about* Keeling's work — useful for showing how the knowledge was eventually understood.

**CO2 press coverage 1955–1965 (E2-05):** **4 results — this is analytically gold.** The Evening Star (DC), January 26, 1958: OCR snippet reads "Mystery of the Warming World" — an article explicitly about global warming, 7 years before the PSAC report, in Washington's daily newspaper. Also: Evening Star August 1959, Atlanta Daily World (Black newspaper) September 1956, Jackson Advocate October 1957. Only 4 newspaper pages in a decade matched this query. **The knowledge was being reported, but barely.**

**Conservation Foundation 1963 (E2-06):** 754 results — broad but noisy. The 1963 CO2 conference was a precursor to the PSAC panel.

### ERA 3: The Warning (1965) — 6/6 returned results

**PSAC report (E3-01):** 2,276 results. The report is buried in a large result set — keyword search returns many environmental policy documents from the 89th Congress era. **The specific "Restoring the Quality of Our Environment" report does not surface in top 5.** This confirms the earlier finding: the document that formally warned a President about CO2 is not easily keyword-discoverable at LC. It exists (it was transmitted to Congress, published by GPO) but requires collection-level browsing or exact-title search.

**PSAC CO2 appendix (E3-02):** 627 results — same pattern. The appendix is the specific climate-relevant section (22 pages, chaired by Revelle).

**LBJ conservation message (E3-03, E3-04):** 3,222 and 1,486 results. Johnson's messages to Congress are well-documented but the climate-specific content is indistinguishable from his broader environmental messaging.

**Newspaper coverage of LBJ's message (E3-05):** **1 result.** One newspaper page in all of Chronicling America matched Johnson + conservation + environment + 1965. The federal government's most significant environmental message — accompanied by a formal scientific warning about CO2 — generated virtually no press coverage that survives in the digitized archive. This is the "silence" finding for 1965.

**Environmental Pollution Panel (E3-06):** 1,650 results. The panel's formal name generates more focused results than "PSAC."

### ERA 4: The Silence (1966–1987) — 7/7 returned results

**Clean Air Act (E4-01):** 1,941 results. Rich congressional record. The Clean Air Act is well-documented; the analytical point is that it addressed visible pollution (smog, particulates) and deliberately excluded CO2.

**Charney Report (E4-02):** 404 results. The 1979 NAS climate assessment should be findable in this set.

**EPA 1983 (E4-03):** 86 results. "Can We Delay a Greenhouse Warming?" — another federal warning, another non-response.

**CO2/greenhouse press 1970–1987 (E4-04):** **1 result.** One newspaper page in 17 years matched "carbon dioxide greenhouse warming climate." Between the PSAC report (1965) and Hansen's testimony (1988), the American public had essentially no newspaper-mediated awareness of climate change.

**Earth Day 1970 (E4-05):** 1,317 results in the general LC collection. But **Earth Day newspaper coverage (E4-06): 1 result.** This is surprising — the first Earth Day was massive. The low count likely reflects Chronicling America's limited coverage of 1970 (digitization tapers off after mid-century). Important scoping note: Chronicling America's newspaper holdings thin dramatically after ~1963.

**Ozone/Montreal Protocol (E4-07):** 27 results. The ozone story — where science *did* lead to policy action — is documented in the congressional record. This is the contrast case.

### ERA 5: The Reckoning (1988–1992) — 5/5 returned results

**Hansen testimony (E5-01):** 306 results. The 1988 testimony is well-documented in the congressional record.

**Senate Energy Committee hearing (E5-02):** 923 results. Tim Wirth's hearing — famously staged on the hottest day of the year with the windows open.

**Global warming press 1988 (E5-03):** **1 result.** This is probably a Chronicling America coverage limitation (post-1963 digitization gap) rather than actual press silence — Hansen's testimony was front-page NYT. But the *absence in the digitized archive* is itself analytically useful: even the most consequential climate moment of the 20th century is barely represented in LC's digitized newspaper holdings.

**IPCC 1990 (E5-04):** 13 results. The first international climate assessment is documented in the congressional record.

**Rio/UNFCCC 1992 (E5-05):** Only 4 results — surprisingly thin for a major international treaty ratified by the Senate.

**Greenhouse effect 1960–1987 (M-02):** **4 results.** Slightly more than "global warming" — the term "greenhouse effect" had marginally more currency in pre-1988 newspapers.

## Analytical Summary: Three Key Findings

### 1. The Federal Government Knew (Rich Congressional Record)
Every era of the evidence chain is documented in the congressional record: IGY appropriations, Revelle's testimony, the PSAC report, Clean Air Act debates, Charney Report, EPA 1983 assessment, Hansen 1988, IPCC, Rio. The institutional knowledge chain is continuous and deep. **LC's congressional holdings are the backbone of this project.**

### 2. The Public Didn't Know (Newspaper Silence)
Chronicling America returns near-zero results for climate-related queries across the entire 1960–1987 period. The gap between institutional knowledge and public awareness is documented in what the digitized newspaper archive does *not* contain. **Caveat:** Chronicling America's digitization coverage thins after ~1963, so post-1963 silence may partly reflect collection limitations rather than actual press silence. Pre-1963 results are more analytically reliable.

### 3. The Specific Documents Are Not Keyword-Discoverable
The 1965 PSAC report — the single most important climate warning document in US history — does not surface in the top results of any of the six searches designed to find it. It exists at LC (transmitted to Congress, published by GPO) but is buried under thousands of generic environmental policy results. **This is the discovery argument for the residency:** the evidence chain exists in LC's collections but requires curatorial assembly to become visible.
